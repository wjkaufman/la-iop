Thesis

Shakespeare takes various artistic liberties within King Henry IV Part 1 to present the royalty in a generally positive light and comment on various social and religious events at the time.

King Henry IV is a historical play, so an understanding of the actual history (certainly at the time in which it was written) is crucial to understanding the play as a whole

The History

Henry IV (1367-1413)

  • Son of John of Gaunt, byname Henry Bolingbroke or Henry of Lancaster
  • Richard II: very unpopular ruler
  • Opposed the crown of Richard II, joined Lords Appelant
  • Richard II seized his inheritence while Henry was banished, then left for campaign in Ireland
  • Henry invaded England, seen as "champion of nobility"
  • Forced Richard II to abdicate, rose to throne in 1399
Try and get through this quickly, because this is information that people already know (to an extent)

Henry V (1386-1422)

  • Son of Henry of Bolingbroke, grew up with Richard II
  • Showed surprising military skill while a teenager fighting in Battle of Shrewsbury
  • Entered government, often opposed his father and challenged his will
  • While Henry IV's health declined, Prince Henry seized control from Henry IV's favorite Thomas Arundel, Archbishop of Canterbury

Other Important History


Sir John Oldcastle (1378-1417)

  • Distinguished soldier, knighted in 1400 by King Henry IV, friends with Prince Henry
  • Member of Lollardy (precursor to Protestant reformation)
  • Archbishop Thomas Arundel called for Oldcastle to be prosecuted
  • King Henry V tried to protect Oldcastle but eventually allowed Arundel to prosecute, condemn Oldcastle
  • Oldcastle killed December of 1417

Deposition of Richard II

King Henry IV's deposition of Richard II is presented in the play, but Shakespeare softens the implications of the deposition through the condemnation of King Richard II as a ruler, or comic relief

Commentary on Richard II

III.ii.60

King Henry IV comments on the superficiality and poor leadership of Richard II

“ The skipping King, he ambled up and down,
With shallow jesters and rash baven wits,
Soon kindled and soon burnt, carded his state,
Mingled his royalty with capering fools…
And in that very line, Harry, standest thou,
For thou hast lost thy princely priveledge
With vile participation…”

Portrayal of Prince Henry

Shakespeare presents Prince Henry as initially troublesome and conflicted with his father, but brings them together through the course of the play to present a united royalty.

Tumultuous Youth

I.ii.145

Prince Henry plans to rob Falstaff with Poins, then reveals his plans to “overthrow” his bad behavior.

“ Well, I'll go with thee. Provide us all things necessary and
meet me tomorrow night in Eastcheap. There I'll sup. Farewell.
…
Yet herein will I imitate the sun,
Who doth permit the base contagious clouds
To smother up his beauty from the world,
That when he please again to be himself,
Being wanted, he may be more wondered at
By breaking through the foul and ugly mists
Of vapours that did seem to strangle him.”

Relationship with King Henry IV

III.ii.125

King Henry IV speaks to Prince Henry in preparation for the Percy rebellion

“ Thou that art like enough, through vassal fear,
Base inclination, and the start of spleen,
To fight against me under Percy's pay,
To dog his heels, and curtsy at his frowns,
To show how much thou art degenerate.”
“ Do not think it so, you shall not find it so;
And God forgive them that so much have swayed
Your majesty's good thoughts away from me!”

Portrayal of John Falstaff

Falstaff is depicted as a contemptible character to negatively portray Oldcastle and Lollardy in general.

Argument against Lollardy

II.iv.370

Prince Henry, acting as his father, describes Falstaff and his character

“ There is a devil haunts thee in the likeness of an old fat man, a tun of man is thy companion… Wherein is he good, but to taste sack and drink it? Wherein neat and cleanly, but to carve a capon and eat it? Wherein cunning, but in craft? Wherein crafty, but in villainy? Wherein villainous, but in all things? Wherein worthy, but in nothing?”
